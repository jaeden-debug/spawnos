# Hillstream Loach: Masters of the Torrent

Hillstream Loaches are one of the most morphologically specialised fish in the freshwater hobby — evolved over millions of years for a single extreme habitat: the fast-flowing, torrential, highly oxygenated highland streams of Southeast Asia. Their flattened body, fused pectoral and pelvic fins forming a ventral suction disc, and ability to cling to smooth rocks in currents that would wash any conventional fish away make them a distinct evolutionary solution to a specific environmental challenge. They are not a fish for standard community aquariums. They require a specialist setup: fast flow, cool temperatures, high oxygen, and hard surfaces covered in biofilm.

For the keeper willing to build their correct environment, Hillstream Loaches are extraordinary showcase fish — flattening against glass with their incredible hydrodynamic profile, displaying fascinating territorial interactions, and grazing continuously on every surface in their tank.

---

## Natural Habitat

Multiple genera are grouped under "Hillstream Loach" in the hobby — primarily *Sewellia*, *Beaufortia*, *Gastromyzon*, *Homaloptera*, and *Pseudogastromyzon*. All share the same essential ecological niche: the splash zone and fast runs of highland streams in Southeast and East Asia.

These streams are characterised by:
- **Extreme current velocity** — Water flow that few other fish can withstand
- **High dissolved oxygen** — Turbulent, fast water equilibrates to near 100% dissolved oxygen saturation
- **Cool temperatures** — Highland origin; 16–22°C is natural; some populations experience as low as 14°C
- **Clean water** — Very low nutrient levels, minimal suspended sediment in pristine habitats
- **Hard, smooth substrate** — Rocks and boulders polished by current
- **Periphyton (biofilm)** — A rich biological crust on all rock surfaces, composed of algae, diatoms, bacteria, and micro-invertebrates — the primary food source

## The Specialised Morphology

Hillstream Loaches have evolved one of the most remarkable body plans in freshwater fish:

**Dorsoventral flattening:** The entire body is flattened top-to-bottom (dorso-ventrally), reducing water resistance in fast flow and allowing close contact with substrate surfaces.

**Paired fin fusion:** The pectoral and pelvic fins are expanded horizontally and positioned symmetrically, forming a continuous suction surface across the fish's underside. This is not a true anatomical sucker (like a pleco's mouth) but rather a hydrodynamic mechanism — the fish holds position using Bernoulli-effect pressure differential between fast water above and still water beneath the fin surface.

**Streamlined head:** The mouth is positioned at the leading edge of the ventral disc, perfectly positioned for scraping biofilm from surfaces while lying flat.

**Reduced swim bladder:** In fast-current specialists, the swim bladder is reduced or modified — these fish do not "swim" in the conventional sense; they glide between holds and reattach using their ventral disc.

## Why Most Hillstream Loach Setups Fail

The most common reason Hillstream Loaches die in captivity:

**Insufficient flow.** Standard aquarium filters and powerheads produce laminar flow — moderate, directional current. Hillstream Loaches require *turbulent, chaotic, high-velocity* flow that replicates torrential stream conditions. A single filter is almost always inadequate.

**Insufficient oxygen.** Fast current carries oxygen. Still tanks are oxygen-poor at the substrate level — exactly where Hillstream Loaches spend all their time. This is compounded by the fact that their cool water requirement conflicts with heat (heat dissolves less oxygen).

**Too warm.** Being sold alongside tropical fish, they're often kept at 25–28°C — temperatures that cause chronic stress and shortened lifespan.

**Biofilm depletion.** Their primary food is the periphyton biofilm that grows on hard surfaces. In standard aquariums this is constantly being cleaned off glass (seen as "algae"). In a Hillstream Loach tank, managed algae/biofilm growth on hardscape surfaces is essential.

## Water Parameters

**Temperature:** 18–24°C. **This is the most critical parameter.** They are cool-water fish. Above 24°C, stress begins; above 26°C, sustained health problems. In temperate climates, an unheated tank may be appropriate. In warmer regions, a chiller may be required.

**pH:** 6.5–7.5. Moderate tolerance. Target neutral.

**Hardness:** 2–12 dGH. Moderately soft to slightly hard. Natural highland streams are soft; some populations in limestone geology encounter harder water.

**Dissolved Oxygen:** Near-saturation. Achieve through turbulent surface agitation, spray bars angled at the surface, or venturi-equipped powerheads.

**Current velocity:** High — multiple times higher than standard aquarium setups. The goal is to create areas of fast flow similar to stream riffles.

## Tank Setup — The Hillstream Biotope

### Size

**Minimum 80 litres**, preferably 120L+. Long, shallow tanks are strongly preferred — a 120 × 40 × 30 cm tank provides excellent flow corridors. Tall tanks don't suit their horizontal lifestyle.

### Flow System

**Flow rate target:** 20–40x tank volume per hour. For an 80L tank, target 1,600–3,200 litres per hour of actual flow, distributed through multiple outlets to create turbulence rather than a single directional stream.

**Equipment:**
- **Wave makers or circular flow powerheads** — Create oscillating, multi-directional current that replicates riffle zones
- **Spray bars** — Positioned at or near the surface to maximise surface agitation and oxygen exchange
- **Multiple powerheads** — Two or three positioned at different angles and heights

### Substrate and Hardscape

**Round river cobbles, slate, smooth river rocks** — The primary living surface. Cover 50–70% of the tank floor with flat and rounded stones. This provides:
- Suction attachment surfaces
- Biofilm cultivation area
- Territory boundaries between individuals
- Visual naturalism

**Fine gravel or sand in non-flow zones** — Secondary substrate in areas of reduced current.

**No sharp gravel or crushed coral** — Their undersides are soft; sharp material causes abrasion.

### Lighting

**Medium to high lighting** to support algae/biofilm cultivation on rocks. Unlike most fish setups where algae is unwanted, in a Hillstream tank, algae and biofilm on rocks is food. Allow a layer of diatoms and green algae to establish on rocks before adding fish.

## Feeding

Hillstream Loaches are **obligate biofilm/periphyton grazers**. Their digestive system is optimised for processing the algae, diatoms, bacteria, and micro-invertebrates of the periphyton layer.

### Primary: Biofilm

In a mature tank with good lighting and moderate nutrients, biofilm grows continuously on rock surfaces. The loaches graze this essentially 24/7 — watch their mouths in action and you can see the scraping motion.

**Pre-seeding rocks:** Before adding Hillstream Loaches to a new tank, allow rocks to develop a biofilm layer by running the tank with lights on for 2–4 weeks. Rocks should have a visible brownish-green coating.

### Supplemental Foods

**Algae wafers** — Sinking wafers provide additional plant-based nutrition. Drop near rock surfaces; loaches will locate and rasp wafers.

**Blanched vegetables** — Zucchini slices, blanched spinach placed on or near rocks. Weight down with a clip or small stone.

**Spirulina-enriched foods** — Flakes or wafers sunk to the substrate zone.

**Live foods:**
- **Daphnia** — Catches some in water column. **[From Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods).**
- **Baby Brine Shrimp** — Nauplii settling on rock surfaces are foraged. **[From Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods).**
- **Micro Worms** — Settle in rock crevices; actively hunted. **[From Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods).**

## Social Behaviour

Hillstream Loaches are **territorial toward conspecifics** — males in particular defend feeding territory on rock surfaces. In undersized tanks with few rocks, constant chasing and stress results.

**Group keeping:** 4–6 individuals in an adequately sized tank with multiple rock structures provides enough territorial subdivision. Pairs or trios in smaller tanks can work if rocks are abundant.

**Interspecific behaviour:** Generally ignore other fish species. They share tank space with fish from compatible cool-water environments peacefully.

---

## Compatible Tankmates

Must share the cool-water, high-flow requirement:

**Excellent companions:**
- **White Cloud Mountain Minnows** (*Tanichthys albonubes*) — Classic cool-water schooling fish; thrive in the same biotope conditions; 20–24°C overlap
- **[Zebra Danio](/species/zebra-danio)** — Tolerates the cooler end of their range; active schoolers that use mid-tank zones well
- Other Hillstream Loach species from the same family — If tank is large enough with sufficient territory
- Smaller Corydoras species that tolerate cooler water (*C. aeneus* to 20°C minimum)

**Avoid:**
- Any tropical fish requiring 26°C+ (temperature conflict)
- Aggressive or territorial fish
- **[Betta Fish](/species/betta-fish)** — Temperature mismatch; Bettas need warmth that harms Hillstream Loaches
- Goldfish (incompatible parameters; large waste production)

## Breeding

Hillstream Loach breeding in captivity is uncommon but documented.

**Known species breeding in captivity:** *Sewellia lineolata* (reticulated hillstream loach) most frequently reported.

**Requirements:**
- Highly mature tank (12+ months) with established biofilm
- Optimal parameters: 20–22°C, very high flow, pH 6.8–7.0
- Multiple individuals including males and females (sexing is difficult; males may have a broader head and more prominent lip region)

**Spawning:** Eggs are deposited on rock surfaces in areas of high current. Males fertilise after female deposits eggs. No parental care.

**Fry:** Extremely small; feed on biofilm from hatching. Separate from adults if raising intentionally. First supplemental food: infusoria and green water, then microworms.
