## Species Overview {#overview}

**Corydoras catfish** (genus *Corydoras*) represent one of the largest and most diverse genera of freshwater fish, with over 170 described species and new species regularly described from South American river systems. They are among the most popular aquarium fish in the world, renowned for their peaceful temperament, utility as "bottom cleaners," and appealing waddling locomotion. What is less commonly appreciated is their complex social behavior, sophisticated communication, and genuinely interesting natural history that spans some of the most diverse aquatic environments in the Amazon basin.

The species most commonly encountered in the trade include *Corydoras aeneus* (bronze cory), *C. paleatus* (peppered cory), *C. sterbai* (Sterba's cory), *C. panda* (panda cory), *C. trilineatus* (three-line cory), and dozens of others across a vast range of patterns and sizes. All share fundamental care requirements that reflect their shared evolutionary biology.

Corydoras belong to the family Callichthyidae — the armored catfish — characterized by two rows of overlapping bony plates (scutes) covering the body instead of scales. This armor is their primary defense against predation and gives them their characteristic feel when handled. Despite the armor, their barbels and skin are sensitive — gravel substrate damages barbels over time, and many chemical treatments are toxic to scaleless or armored catfish at doses safe for scaled fish.

## Natural Habitat {#natural-habitat}

### Geographic and Ecological Range

The genus *Corydoras* is distributed across virtually all of South America east of the Andes — from the Orinoco drainage in Venezuela, through the vast Amazon basin, to the La Plata system in Argentina. This extraordinary geographic range corresponds to an equally extraordinary range of habitats:

- **Lowland tropical forest streams** — warm, soft, acidic blackwater
- **Andean foothill streams** — cooler, clearer, faster-flowing
- **Coastal plain rivers** — often harder, near-neutral to slightly alkaline
- **Flooded seasonally in várzea forest** — experiencing dramatic water level fluctuations
- **Tidal zone river margins** — some species tolerate brackish influence

This range of habitats is why different *Corydoras* species have meaningfully different water chemistry requirements — *C. sterbai* from the Rio Guaporé region needs warm water (26–30°C), while *C. paleatus* from subtropical Argentina tolerates 16–22°C and would be stressed at tropical temperatures.

### Water Chemistry

Most commonly kept *Corydoras* species prefer:
- **Temperature:** 22–26°C (72–79°F) — species-dependent
- **pH:** 6.0–7.5 — soft to near-neutral water
- **GH:** 2–12 dGH — soft to moderately hard
- **KH:** 1–6 dKH — low buffering
- **TDS:** 50–200 ppm

Many wild *Corydoras* habitats are blackwater or blackwater-influenced — tannic, humic acid-rich, and soft. This is particularly true of the species most prized by serious cory keepers: *C. sterbai*, *C. adolfoi*, *C. duplicareus*, and others from upper Amazonian blackwater tributaries.

> The [Blackwater Aquatics Knowledge Base](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/blogs/knowledge-base) covers blackwater aquarium chemistry in depth — essential reading for keeping upper Amazonian *Corydoras* species optimally.

### The Intestinal Breathing Adaptation

Like bettas, many *Corydoras* species can supplement gill respiration with intestinal breathing — they gulp air at the surface and extract oxygen through a highly vascularized portion of the intestine. This adaptation evolved as a response to seasonally oxygen-depleted environments (warm, shallow, vegetation-choked pools during the dry season). In captivity, a cory that frequently "rockets" to the surface to gulp air is signaling low dissolved oxygen — increase surface agitation or check temperature (warm water holds less DO).

## Aquarium Care Requirements {#aquarium-care}

### The Non-Negotiable: Group Size and Substrate

Two aspects of corydoras care are absolutely non-negotiable:

**1. Group size minimum 6.** Corydoras are obligate schooling fish with complex social behavior. Kept alone or in pairs, they show visible stress behaviors: hiding constantly, reduced activity, refusal to eat, shortened lifespan. A school of 6+ of the same species produces cohesive schooling behavior, active exploration, and the full behavioral repertoire these fish exhibit. A school of 10–12 is better.

**2. Sand substrate only.** Corydoras barbels are sensory organs — highly innervated and essential for locating food buried in the substrate. On gravel, repeated contact with rough edges causes barbel erosion (the barbels shorten and fray), bacterial infection in damaged barbel tissue, and a fish that can no longer feed efficiently. **Fine sand is not optional for corydoras — it is a health requirement.**

### Water Parameters

| Parameter | Optimal Range | Acceptable Range |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Temperature | 22–26°C (72–79°F) | 18–28°C (species-dep.) |
| pH | 6.0–7.5 | 5.5–8.0 |
| GH | 2–12 dGH | 1–18 dGH |
| KH | 1–6 dKH | 0–10 dKH |
| Ammonia | 0 ppm | — |
| Nitrite | 0 ppm | — |
| Nitrate | <20 ppm | <30 ppm |

**Important:** Corydoras are highly sensitive to many medications and salt. Any treatment containing salt should be used with extreme caution or avoided entirely — salt stress is a well-documented problem in scaleless and armored catfish. Many fish disease treatments specify a reduced dose for "scaleless fish" — always follow this guidance for corydoras.

> Use the [Salt Dosage Calculator](/tools/salt-dosage) and always check the scaleless fish warning before any salt treatment in a tank containing corydoras.

## Tank Setup Guide {#tank-setup}

### The Ideal Corydoras Environment

**Substrate:** Fine sand, 2–4 cm deep. Pool filter sand, play sand (rinsed thoroughly), or commercial fine aquarium sand all work. Dark sand makes the fish's patterns more visible and is more natural-looking.

**Current:** Corydoras appreciate gentle to moderate current — many species come from clear streams with some flow. A powerhead directed along one side of the tank, or a canister outlet creating a gentle circular current, provides enrichment and exercise. However, avoid strong turbulent flow that exhausts the fish.

**Cover:** Dense planting provides security. Corydoras school actively under open conditions but appreciate the option to retreat to vegetation or structured hides. Java Fern, *Anubias*, and *Vallisneria* planted densely create an appropriate environment.

**Hiding spots:** Caves, ceramic pots, and structured hides. Corydoras are not highly territorial but appreciate enclosed resting spots.

**Lighting:** Moderate. Corydoras are active during the day but in dimmer conditions — they are not cryptic like nocturnal catfish, but they prefer moderate light to intense illumination.

## Feeding Guide {#feeding}

Corydoras are benthic omnivores, foraging continuously through the substrate for organic particles, invertebrates, and detritus.

**Important:** Corydoras must be fed specifically — they cannot compete effectively with mid-column and surface feeders for food. Floating flake food is largely inaccessible to them. **Sinking foods are essential.**

**Recommended foods:**
- High-quality sinking wafers or pellets (Hikari Sinking Wafers, NorthFin Bottom Feeder Pellets)
- Sinking algae wafers — corydoras consume vegetable matter readily
- Frozen foods thawed and allowed to sink: bloodworms, Daphnia, brine shrimp
- Sinking pellets dropped after lights-out (corydoras are more active at dusk)

**Live foods from [Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods):**
- **Live Daphnia** — natural food; corydoras hunt Daphnia in the water column and on the substrate; excellent digestive health benefits
- **Live Scuds (Gammarus)** — outstanding enrichment food for adult corydoras; added to the tank they actively hunt them across the substrate
- **Microworms** — essential for corydoras fry; the tiny nematodes reach the substrate where fry feed

Feed corydoras at dusk or just after lights-out for best uptake.

## Species Guide: Popular Corydoras Species

### *Corydoras aeneus* — Bronze Cory
The most common and hardy species. Olive-gold body with metallic sheen. Tolerates a wide range of parameters. Best beginner cory. Temperature: 20–28°C.

### *Corydoras paleatus* — Peppered Cory
Pale grey with dark irregular marbling. From subtropical Argentina; prefers cooler water (18–24°C) than most. One of the easiest to breed.

### *Corydoras sterbai* — Sterba's Cory
Orange pectoral fins, white-spotted dark body. Warm water specialist (26–30°C). Beautiful and popular. Often kept with discus due to shared temperature requirements.

### *Corydoras panda* — Panda Cory
White body with black eye mask and black caudal spot. Sensitive to poor water quality; needs pristine conditions. Maximum 5cm.

### *Corydoras habrosus* / *C. hastatus* / *C. pygmaeus* — Pygmy Corydoras
Tiny (2–3cm). These miniature species are mid-water swimmers as much as bottom dwellers — they school in open water. Require groups of 10+ minimum.

## Breeding Guide {#breeding}

Corydoras breeding is achievable with attention to conditioning and water chemistry. Most species are egg-scatterers, attaching adhesive eggs to plant leaves and glass.

**Conditioning:** Feed heavily with live foods — particularly [live baby brine shrimp](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods) and [Daphnia from Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods) — for 3–4 weeks. The male-to-female ratio of 2:1 or 3:1 males:females produces reliable spawning attempts.

**Triggering spawning:** A cool water change (water 3–5°C cooler than the tank) mimics the onset of Amazonian rainy season. Repeat over 2–3 days. Spawning typically follows within 24–48 hours of the third cool water change.

**Egg care:** Remove eggs to a separate container with gentle aeration and antifungal (methylene blue or almond leaf extract). Eggs hatch in 4–6 days.

**Fry first foods:** [Microworms](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods) for the first 1–2 weeks, transitioning to [baby brine shrimp nauplii](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods). Both available from Blackwater Aquatics.

## Compatibility {#compatibility}

Corydoras are among the most universally compatible aquarium fish. They are peaceful with virtually all non-predatory species and are strongly recommended as community tank members.

**Excellent companions:** [Neon tetras](/species/neon-tetra), [angelfish](/species/angelfish), [guppies](/species/guppy), [betta fish](/species/betta-fish) (bettas rarely bother corydoras due to their armor), [cherry shrimp](/species/cherry-shrimp), small rasboras.

**Avoid:** Aggressive cichlids that may harass them; very large predatory fish; salt in the tank (see medication note above).
