## Species Overview {#overview}

The **neon tetra** (*Paracheirodon innesi*) is arguably the most iconic freshwater aquarium fish in the world. The electric blue horizontal stripe running from nose to adipose fin, paired with the vivid red stripe covering the posterior half of the body, has made this small characid recognizable even to people who have never kept fish. Globally, hundreds of millions of neon tetras are produced and sold annually — it is the single highest-volume ornamental fish in the trade.

Despite its ubiquity, the neon tetra is surprisingly misunderstood. It is regularly kept in conditions entirely wrong for the species — hard, alkaline tap water, warm tropical community tanks, overstocked aquariums — and suffers accordingly. Understanding the natural origins of this fish transforms its care from routine to purposeful.

The genus *Paracheirodon* contains three species: *P. innesi* (neon tetra), *P. axelrodi* (cardinal tetra), and *P. simulans* (green neon tetra). All three are closely related, share similar care requirements, and are all Amazonian blackwater fish — though the cardinal tetra is the more extreme blackwater specialist and maintains its red stripe along the full body length (a useful visual distinction).

*Paracheirodon innesi* was first described by Myers in 1936 from specimens collected by A.J. Randle in the Loreto region of Peru. The species is named after William T. Innes, publisher of the influential *Aquarium* magazine and author of *Exotic Aquarium Fishes* — one of the most important popularizing texts in 20th-century American fishkeeping.

## Natural Habitat {#natural-habitat}

### The Blackwater Rivers of South America

Wild neon tetras inhabit the upper tributaries of the Solimões and Putumayo river systems in western Amazonia — primarily in Peru, Colombia, and western Brazil. Their habitat consists of small, slow-moving to standing blackwater streams and pools in equatorial rainforest, characterized by:

- **Extreme tannin staining** — water the color of strong tea or coffee from decomposing leaf litter and humic acids leaching from forest floor vegetation
- **Very low pH:** 4.0–6.5 — genuinely acidic water, far more acidic than most community aquariums
- **Very soft water:** GH 0–4 dGH — some blackwater streams have essentially no measurable hardness
- **Very low conductivity / TDS:** 10–60 ppm — the water is essentially mineral-free, composed primarily of pure rainwater filtered through leaf litter
- **Low light:** The dense rainforest canopy allows minimal light penetration to the forest floor streams; water clarity is further reduced by tannin staining
- **Moderate temperature:** 22–26°C (72–79°F) — cooler than the open-river lowland Amazonian mainstream

This is the habitat that shaped the neon tetra's biology. The bioluminescent-looking blue and red stripe is not for visibility in clear water — it is a communication signal in near-dark, tannin-blackened conditions where the stripe glows with remarkable intensity.

### The Blackwater Chemistry Explanation

Blackwater chemistry is characterized by high concentrations of **humic acids** and **fulvic acids** — organic compounds produced by the decomposition of plant material. These compounds:

- Dramatically lower pH through displacement of carbonate alkalinity and hydrogen ion donation
- Remove or chelate mineral ions, resulting in extremely soft, low-TDS water
- Have documented antimicrobial, antifungal, and anti-inflammatory properties
- Absorb ultraviolet light, creating the characteristic amber to dark-brown water color
- Interact with fish skin and mucus in ways that reduce osmotic stress and support slime coat integrity

Understanding this explains why neon tetras in hard, alkaline, mineral-rich tap water are chronically stressed — the chemistry is fundamentally incompatible with their evolved physiology. It also explains why tannin additions (Indian almond leaves, driftwood, botanicals) dramatically improve neon tetra health in captive settings.

## Aquarium Care Requirements {#aquarium-care}

### Water Parameters

| Parameter | Optimal Range | Acceptable Range | Avoid |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|-------|
| Temperature | 22–25°C (72–77°F) | 20–28°C | >28°C sustained |
| pH | 5.5–7.0 | 5.0–7.5 | >8.0 |
| General Hardness (GH) | 1–6 dGH | 1–12 dGH | >18 dGH |
| Carbonate Hardness (KH) | 1–4 dKH | 0–6 dKH | — |
| Ammonia | 0 ppm | — | >0.1 ppm |
| Nitrite | 0 ppm | — | >0.25 ppm |
| Nitrate | <10 ppm | <20 ppm | >40 ppm |

Neon tetras are **more sensitive to water quality than many commonly kept fish**. The "neon tetra disease" often discussed in the hobby (a wasting disease caused by *Pleistophora hyphessobryconis*) is strongly associated with chronic stress from inappropriate water chemistry and overcrowding — not an inevitable feature of the species.

> Use the [GH/KH Hardness Converter](/tools/gh-kh-converter) to verify your water hardness matches the soft-water requirements of neon tetras.

### Group Size and Stocking

Neon tetras are obligate schooling fish. **Minimum group: 8–10 individuals.** Fewer than 6 produces visible stress behaviors: erratic swimming, hiding, failure to school cohesively, susceptibility to disease. A school of 15–20 neons in a well-planted tank is one of the most beautiful sights in freshwater fishkeeping.

> Use the [Stocking Density Calculator](/tools/stocking-density) to determine how many neon tetras are appropriate for your tank volume alongside other species.

### Minimum Tank Size

20 litres (5 US gallons) is the absolute minimum for a nano group. A proper school of 12–15 neons requires at least 60–80 litres (15–20 gallons) with appropriate planting and structure.

## Tank Setup Guide {#tank-setup}

### The Blackwater Biotope Approach

A blackwater biotope tank for neon tetras is one of the most rewarding aquarium projects in freshwater fishkeeping. It replicates the visual and chemical environment of the wild habitat:

**Substrate:** Dark sand or fine dark gravel; covers the substrate with a layer of dried leaf litter (Indian almond leaves, dried magnolia leaves, oak leaves). The leaves decompose slowly, releasing tannins and providing microfauna habitat.

**Botanicals:** Seed pods (catappa pods, Cariniana pods, Cyperus pods), driftwood (especially spiderwood and Malaysian driftwood which release significant tannins), and botanicals from the Blackwater Aquatics collection.

**Plants:** Low-light species adapted to soft, acidic conditions: *Echinodorus* (Amazon sword), *Cryptocoryne* spp. (particularly *C. wendtii* and *C. parva*), Java Fern, *Blyxa japonica*, *Cabomba furcata*, *Rotala macrandra* (in moderate light).

**Lighting:** Subdued. A 2700–4000K spectrum LED at low intensity for 8–10 hours per day recreates the dappled understory light of the Amazonian forest floor. Strong lighting washes out the neon tetra's colors and increases algae growth.

**Filtration:** Gentle sponge filter or low-flow canister. Neon tetras come from near-still water; strong current is stressful. A filter providing 3–5x tank volume turnover per hour is appropriate, with output diffused.

### Enhancing Neon Tetra Color

The neon tetra's blue stripe is produced by **iridophores** — cells containing crystalline platelets that reflect light at specific wavelengths through structural coloration, not pigment. The color intensity varies with: angle of observation, light intensity, fish health, and stress level. A healthy, unstressed neon tetra in dim, blackened water with appropriate conspecifics glows electric blue with far more intensity than the same fish under bright light in a bare tank.

For maximum color display: soft acidic water, dim warm-spectrum lighting, dark substrate, tannin staining, and a confident school of 15+ individuals.

## Feeding Guide {#feeding}

Neon tetras are micro-predators and omnivores in the wild, consuming primarily small invertebrates, zooplankton, and occasional plant matter.

**Recommended foods:**
- Quality micro-pellets (0.5–1mm) — sized appropriately for their small mouths
- Frozen micro foods: bloodworms (crushed or chopped), Daphnia, brine shrimp, cyclops
- Live foods from [Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods):
  - **Live Daphnia** — a natural food source and superb conditioning food; neons will hunt Daphnia continuously
  - **Live Baby Brine Shrimp** — excellent for growth and color conditioning
  - **Microworms** — essential first food for neon tetra fry

Feed small amounts twice daily. Neons have small mouths — particle size matters. Large pellets or thick food strips are wasted and pollute the water.

## Compatibility {#compatibility}

Neon tetras are peaceful, community-appropriate fish. Avoid:

- Any fish large enough to eat them (most cichlids, large tetras, oscar, discus in small tanks)
- Aggressive or fin-nipping species
- Fish with very different water temperature requirements

**Excellent companions:** [Corydoras](/species/corydoras), [guppies](/species/guppy), small rasboras, [kuhli loaches](/species/kuhli-loach), dwarf gouramis, [cherry shrimp](/species/cherry-shrimp) (with caution — neons may occasionally pick at juvenile shrimp).

## Breeding Guide {#breeding}

Breeding neon tetras is considered challenging — they require soft, acidic water (pH 5.5–6.5, GH <3) that many keepers cannot produce from tap water. However, it is achievable with RO water or rainwater blended to target parameters.

Condition a breeding pair with [live Daphnia](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods) and [baby brine shrimp](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods) for 2–3 weeks. Set up a small (10–15 litre) breeding tank with blackwater conditions, fine-leaved plants (Java moss, *Cabomba*), very dim or no light, and a spawning mop.

At dusk, the pair will scatter adhesive eggs among fine-leaved plants. Remove adults after spawning. Eggs hatch in 24 hours; larvae are free-swimming by day 5. First food: [infusoria and microworms](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods), transitioning to baby brine shrimp at 10–14 days.

## Health and Disease {#health}

**Neon Tetra Disease (NTD):** Caused by the microsporidian parasite *Pleistophora hyphessobryconis*. Symptoms: fading of the blue stripe (the first visible sign), white cyst-like patches in muscle tissue, curved spine, wasting. **There is no cure.** Affected fish must be removed and euthanized humanely to prevent spread. Prevention: quarantine all new fish, maintain excellent water quality, avoid overcrowding, use soft acidic water.

**False NTD** (bacterial): Resembles NTD but caused by bacterial infection. May respond to antibiotics if caught early. Distinguished from true NTD by lack of cyst formation visible in the muscle.

**Ich:** Responds well to standard treatment; raise temperature to 28°C and treat with appropriate medication.
