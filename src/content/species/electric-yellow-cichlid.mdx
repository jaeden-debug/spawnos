# Electric Yellow Cichlid: The Complete Lake Malawi Care Guide

The electric yellow cichlid is the friendly face of the African Rift Lake hobby — a glowing lemon-yellow mbuna with crisp black fin edges that brings the colour of a marine fish to a hard-water freshwater tank. *Labidochromis caeruleus* (the "yellow lab") is among the most peaceful and beginner-friendly of the Lake Malawi mbuna, and its solid, saturated yellow makes it one of the most popular African cichlids in the world. Keeping it well means embracing a different style of fishkeeping: hard, alkaline water, a rocky aquascape, group dynamics, and a vegetable-forward diet.

This guide is the complete reference: the yellow lab's biology and Rift Lake origins, the hard alkaline water and rockwork it needs, the herbivore-leaning diet that prevents the dreaded Malawi bloat, how to manage mbuna group dynamics, its fascinating mouthbrooding, and which tank mates suit it.

---

## Species Overview

The electric yellow cichlid (*Labidochromis caeruleus*), or yellow lab, is a **mbuna** — one of the rock-dwelling cichlids of Lake Malawi — reaching about 10 cm (4 inches). Its most popular form is a solid, electric lemon-yellow with **black margins** on the dorsal and other fins (males show stronger black, including a black band through the dorsal). The species name *caeruleus* means "blue," reflecting a blue wild form, but the yellow morph utterly dominates the hobby.

The yellow lab is **one of the most peaceful mbuna** — a relative term, as mbuna are generally semi-aggressive — making it a favourite gateway into the African Rift Lake hobby. It is **hardy**, **strikingly coloured**, **long-lived** (6–10 years), and a **maternal mouthbrooder** whose breeding is fascinating to watch. It is rated intermediate because it needs a specific environment (hard, alkaline water and rockwork), is kept in groups with their own social dynamics, and requires a vegetable-forward diet to avoid digestive disease. For keepers willing to set up a dedicated Malawi tank, it's one of the most rewarding African cichlids.

## Natural History and Origin

*Labidochromis caeruleus* is endemic to **Lake Malawi**, one of the African Great Rift Lakes, where it lives among the rocky shorelines as part of the **mbuna** group — small, colourful, rock-dwelling cichlids that graze the algae-covered rocks (the "aufwuchs") and the tiny invertebrates within it. Lake Malawi is famous for its hard, highly alkaline, mineral-rich, stable water and its explosive diversity of cichlids, and recreating that environment is the foundation of keeping any mbuna.

Mbuna are **territorial and social**, living in dense, competitive communities on the rocks, which is why they're kept in groups in heavily-rocked tanks where aggression is spread among many individuals rather than focused on one. As maternal mouthbrooders, female mbuna incubate their eggs and fry inside their mouths — a remarkable strategy that protects the young in a predator-rich lake. The yellow lab is a relatively gentle, micro-predator/grazer mbuna, less purely herbivorous than some, but still requiring the vegetable-forward diet typical of the group.

## Water Parameters — Hard and Alkaline

This is non-negotiable: mbuna need hard, alkaline water utterly unlike a soft-water community tank.

| Parameter | Target | Notes |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Temperature | 76–82°F (24–28°C) | Stable Rift Lake range. |
| pH | 7.6–8.8 | **Alkaline** — a defining requirement. |
| Hardness (GH) | 10–25 dGH | **Hard**, mineral-rich water. |
| Carbonate hardness (KH) | 10–20 dKH | High KH buffers the high, stable pH. |
| Ammonia / Nitrite | 0 ppm | Keep the tank cycled. |
| Nitrate | < 30 ppm | Keep reasonable with water changes. |

The yellow lab needs **hard, alkaline, mineral-rich water** with a stable high pH — the opposite of the soft, acidic conditions that suit Amazonian fish. In soft-water areas, keepers raise hardness and pH with crushed coral or aragonite substrate, Rift Lake salt/mineral mixes, or buffering products. The high KH keeps the pH stable and high. Confirm cycling with the [nitrogen cycle tracker](/tools/nitrogen-cycle), and use the [GH/KH converter](/tools/gh-kh-converter) and [water parameters reference](/tools/water-parameters) to dial in and maintain hard, alkaline conditions. Never mix mbuna into a soft-water community.

## Tank Setup Guide

### Tank size

A group of yellow labs (and mbuna are best kept in groups) needs a **minimum of 40 gallons (150 litres)**, with 55+ gallons strongly preferred for a proper mbuna community. The larger footprint provides the territories and swimming room that diffuse aggression.

### Aquascape — rock is everything

Build a tank dominated by **rockwork** — stacked rocks, caves, and crevices that create abundant territories, hiding places, and broken sightlines. This rocky structure is essential to mbuna keeping: it mimics their habitat and is the primary tool for managing aggression, giving every fish places to claim and to flee to. Use an **alkaline substrate** (aragonite/crushed coral) to help buffer the water. Plants are largely incompatible — mbuna dig and most plants dislike the hard alkaline water — though tough plants like anubias on rock can sometimes work.

### Filtration, flow, lid

Mbuna are active and produce significant waste, so use **robust, over-rated filtration** and good flow, with disciplined water changes to keep nitrate down. A lid is recommended, as cichlids jump.

## Feeding Guide — Vegetable-Forward, Avoid Bloat

Diet is a genuine health issue for mbuna. Many graze algae and plant matter in the wild, and a **vegetable-forward diet is essential** to avoid the digestive disease known as **Malawi bloat**, which is triggered by too much rich, fatty, protein-heavy food.

### What to feed

- **Spirulina- and algae-based pellets/flakes ("Malawi" or cichlid herbivore formulas)** — the staple.
- **Vegetable matter** — blanched vegetables and algae round out the diet.
- **Sparing protein** — the yellow lab is more of an omnivore/micro-predator than some pure-herbivore mbuna, so a little frozen food (e.g., occasional [daphnia](/species/daphnia) or mysis) is fine, but **avoid rich, fatty foods** like excessive bloodworm or beefheart, which cause bloat.

### How often

Feed **once or twice daily**, modest amounts of vegetable-forward food. **Do not overfeed**, and avoid protein-heavy diets — Malawi bloat (loss of appetite, swelling, stringy white faeces, rapid breathing) is often fatal and is largely a diet-and-water-quality disease. A correct herbivore-leaning diet keeps yellow labs healthy and brilliantly coloured.

## Behavior and Temperament

Electric yellow labs are **among the most peaceful mbuna**, but "peaceful mbuna" still means semi-aggressive by community standards — they are territorial, especially males, and live in a competitive group hierarchy. Kept correctly in a group within a heavily-rocked tank, their aggression is spread out and manageable, and they're a relatively calm, gregarious presence compared with feistier mbuna.

The keys to harmony are **group keeping** (a group, ideally with more females than males, spreads aggression), **abundant rockwork** (territories and refuges), and **adequate space**. Males display and spar over territory and females; brooding females retreat to incubate. Their relative gentleness, stunning colour, and active behaviour make yellow labs the ideal first mbuna and a backbone species of mixed Malawi communities.

## Compatibility

Yellow labs belong in a **dedicated hard-water Lake Malawi / mbuna community**, not a soft-water tropical tank.

**Good tank mates:** other **peaceful-to-moderate mbuna** (kept as a proper mbuna community with many individuals to spread aggression), *Synodontis* catfish, and other Rift Lake fish that share the hard alkaline water and robust temperament.

**Poor/incompatible tank mates:**

- **Soft-water community fish** — wrong water chemistry and too delicate for mbuna.
- **Very aggressive mbuna** — overwhelm the gentler yellow lab in small tanks.
- **Shy or slow fish** — outcompeted and stressed.
- **Most plants and small invertebrates** — incompatible with mbuna behaviour and water.

The right approach is a **species-appropriate Malawi setup**. ## Breeding Guide

Electric yellow labs are **maternal mouthbrooders**, and breeding them is one of the most fascinating spectacles in the cichlid hobby.

**Sexing** can be subtle: males typically show stronger black fin markings (including a bold black band in the dorsal) and may be slightly larger and bolder; females are often a softer yellow with less black. Keeping a group ensures both sexes and natural pairing.

A male displays and courts a female over his territory; she lays eggs and **immediately takes them into her mouth**, where they are fertilised (using the male's "egg-spot" markings to ensure fertilisation) and then **incubated in her mouth for around three weeks**. During this time she doesn't eat, her throat visibly bulging, and she shelters in the rocks. She eventually releases fully-formed, free-swimming fry, which she may briefly continue to shelter in her mouth when threatened. The fry are large and easy to raise on crushed flake, powdered foods, and [baby brine shrimp](/species/baby-brine-shrimp). Many keepers move brooding females to a separate tank to protect the fry, or strip and artificially raise them. Watching a female mouthbrood is a remarkable window into one of nature's most protective parenting strategies.

## Health and Disease

Yellow labs are hardy when kept in correct water, with diet-related illness the main species-specific concern.

**Malawi bloat** is the signature mbuna disease — abdominal swelling, loss of appetite, stringy white faeces, lethargy, and rapid breathing — triggered by inappropriate (too rich/fatty/protein-heavy) diet, poor water quality, and stress; it progresses fast and is often fatal, so **prevention through a correct herbivore-leaning diet and clean water is essential**. **Ich** can follow temperature swings. **Bacterial infections** follow poor water or injury. Aggression injuries can occur if the tank is under-rocked or overstocked with the wrong mix.

Prevention: **hard, alkaline, stable, clean water**; robust filtration and water changes; a **vegetable-forward diet, avoiding rich foods**; group keeping with ample rockwork; and quarantine of new arrivals. Given those, the yellow lab is a robust, long-lived, brilliantly-coloured cichlid.

## Interesting Facts

- **Marine colour in fresh water.** Its solid electric yellow rivals reef fish, all in a hard-water freshwater tank.
- **Mouthbrooding mothers.** Females incubate eggs and fry in their mouths for ~3 weeks, not eating the whole time — one of nature's most protective parenting strategies.
- **Egg-spots and fertilisation.** Male mbuna have spot markings on the anal fin that play a role in the mouthbrooding fertilisation "trick."
- **The gentle mbuna.** Among the most peaceful of the Lake Malawi rock cichlids, making it the ideal first African cichlid.
- **A name that says blue.** *Labidochromis caeruleus* means "blue," from a wild blue form — but the yellow morph rules the hobby.

The electric yellow cichlid is the perfect introduction to the African Rift Lake hobby: a brilliant, hardy, relatively peaceful mbuna that brings marine-grade colour to a hard-water freshwater tank. Success means embracing the mbuna style — **hard, alkaline, stable water**, a tank dominated by **rockwork**, **group keeping** to diffuse aggression, and a **vegetable-forward diet** that avoids the fatal Malawi bloat. Set up a dedicated 40-gallon-plus Malawi tank along these lines, keep yellow labs in a group with appropriate mbuna tank mates, and you'll enjoy glowing yellow cichlids for years — and very likely witness the remarkable spectacle of a mouthbrooding female.
