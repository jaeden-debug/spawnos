# Pea Puffer: The Curious Nano Predator

The Pea Puffer (*Carinotetraodon travancoricus*) is the smallest pufferfish in the world — barely 2.5 cm fully grown — and one of the most personality-dense freshwater fish per centimetre in the hobby. They investigate everything in their tank, track their keeper's movements through the glass, change colour subtly with mood, and pursue prey with a precision that belies their tiny size. They are also challenging: strict carnivores that reject dry foods, territorial enough to shred tankmates' fins, and endemic to a specific geographic region now under significant habitat pressure.

---

## Natural History and Conservation Status

Pea Puffers are endemic to the Western Ghats biodiversity hotspot of southwest India — specifically the river systems of Kerala and Karnataka including the Pamba River. Their natural habitat consists of densely vegetated, slow-moving freshwater rivers with abundant macrophytes, submerged vegetation, and significant invertebrate populations.

The species faces pressure from habitat degradation (agricultural chemical runoff, river alteration) and collection for the aquarium trade. Kerala populations are particularly stressed. Wild-caught specimens are still common in the hobby, but captive breeding is increasingly important and successfully practised.

**IUCN status:** Vulnerable.

---

## The Puffer Physiology

Pufferfish share several unusual biological traits:

**Teeth:** A fused beak-like dentition (derived from fused teeth) that can crush invertebrate shells — snail shells, small clam shells, crustacean exoskeletons. This is why hard-shelled live foods are important for dental wear management. Overgrown teeth (a real problem in captivity) prevent proper feeding.

**Inflation:** Like all puffers, Pea Puffers can inflate their bodies with water (or air if foolishly removed from water) as a predator deterrent. This is extremely stressful and should never be intentionally induced.

**Skin:** Puffers lack scales — their skin is smooth or covered in small prickles. This makes them more vulnerable to parasites and sensitive to many medications.

**Eyesight:** Excellent — each eye moves independently, giving near-360° vision. They track movement exceptionally well, which contributes to their "intelligent" appearance.

**Tetrodotoxin:** Wild pufferfish accumulate tetrodotoxin in organs. Captive-bred aquarium puffers raised on safe foods typically have negligible toxin levels, but their flesh should not be consumed.

## Water Parameters

**Temperature:** 22–28°C. Target 24–26°C for daily life. Breeding attempts benefit from the upper range (26–28°C).

**pH:** 6.5–7.5. Moderate tolerance. Target neutral to slightly acidic (6.8–7.2).

**Hardness:** 5–15 dGH. Moderate hardness reflects their Indian river origin, which is harder and less tannin-stained than many Southeast Asian or Amazonian blackwater setups.

**Ammonia/Nitrite:** Zero. Pea Puffers are sensitive to ammonia spikes.

**Nitrate:** Below 20 ppm. Planted tanks assist significantly with nitrate control.

## Tank Setup

### Size

**Minimum 40 litres** for a single Pea Puffer. Many keepers start with 60–80L which provides more territory and better water stability.

**Stocking density:** A common recommendation is **1 Pea Puffer per 10–15 litres** of tank volume in species-only setups. A 60L tank can hold 4–6 comfortably with enough plant structure to break sightlines. Males are more territorial than females — a ratio of 2–3 females per male is recommended.

### Plants — The Key to Social Management

Dense planting is mandatory for managing Pea Puffer aggression. Visual barriers between individuals reduce constant territorial chasing:

- **Cryptocoryne** — Dense mid-ground plants that create ground-level territory boundaries
- **Java Moss** — Provides hunting cover and spawning sites
- **Floating plants** (Salvinia, Frogbit) — Diffuse lighting and provide topwater cover
- **Anubias and Java Fern** — Attached to driftwood; creates vertical territory separation
- **Hornwort / Ceratophyllum** — Fast-growing stem plants that quickly fill open spaces

A tank that's 60–70% plant coverage (by visual density) is the target. This sounds dense but Pea Puffers actively use every plant structure.

### Substrate and Décor

Fine sand or smooth small gravel. Driftwood and botanical elements create visual complexity and provide hunting terrain.

**Snail culture in a separate container** is highly recommended — live snails are the best food for dental health and natural feeding stimulation (see feeding section).

## Feeding — The Most Critical Aspect of Care

**Pea Puffers are obligate carnivores.** They will typically refuse all dry foods — pellets, flake, freeze-dried. Attempting to sustain them on dry food leads to slow starvation. Keepers must provide live and frozen invertebrate foods.

### Primary Foods

**Ramshorn Snails (live)** — The single most important food. Pea Puffers pursue snails with intense focus, using their beak to crush shells. The shell-crushing activity wears teeth naturally, preventing overgrowth. A steady supply of small ramshorn snails (2–5mm) is ideal. Culture your own in a separate container — they breed prolifically on algae and vegetable scraps.

**Bladder Snails (live)** — Easier to culture than ramshorn; similar benefit. Both species multiply rapidly with minimal effort.

**Malaysian Trumpet Snails** — Harder shell; good for larger/more mature puffers.

**Frozen Bloodworm** — Accepted readily; excellent nutritional supplement. Use as part of a varied diet, not the sole food.

**Frozen Daphnia** — High digestive fibre; useful as dietary variation.

**Frozen Mysis Shrimp** — High protein; relished by most puffers.

**Live Baby Brine Shrimp** — Excellent for small/young puffers and conditioning for breeding. **[Order live BBS from Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods).**

**Live Micro Worms** — Good for fry and juveniles. **[Available at Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods).**

**Live Daphnia** — Puffers hunt these in the water column enthusiastically. Excellent enrichment behaviour. **[Available at Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods).**

> **Explore live food cultivation on the [Blackwater Aquatics Knowledge Base](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/blogs/knowledge-base).**

### Dental Overgrowth

Without regular hard-shell foods (snails), Pea Puffer teeth overgrow until the beak can no longer open properly. Signs: inability to close mouth fully, difficulty eating, progressive weight loss. Dental trimming by an experienced keeper (using small scissors under anaesthesia) is possible but stressful. Prevention is far preferable — maintain a steady snail supply.

### Feeding Frequency

2 small feedings per day. Feed until the belly is visibly rounded but not distended. An overfed puffer will have a noticeable "round belly" — a healthy sign. A sunken belly indicates underfeeding.

## Behaviour and Temperament

**Intelligence:** Pea Puffers display remarkably complex behaviour for their size. They recognise individual human faces (or at least silhouettes) and respond differently to regular feeders vs. strangers. They investigate tank changes immediately and methodically.

**Aggression:** Males in particular are territorial. They develop colour intensity when asserting dominance — darkening their mid-body stripe. Persistent chasing of one individual by another indicates territory imbalance — add more plants or separate.

**Colour:** Mood and health visible through colouration:
- **Bright yellow-green with dark spots:** Healthy, content
- **Faded, pale:** Stressed, ill, or cold
- **Dark mid-body stripe intensified:** Aggression or breeding display in males
- **White belly after feeding:** Normal — post-feeding colour shift

**Fin nipping:** Pea Puffers are notorious fin nippers. Long-finned, slow-moving fish will be targeted relentlessly. Do not keep with: Bettas, Guppies with long fins, Angelfish, or any slow or long-finned species.

## Tankmates

**Species-only setups are strongly recommended** for beginners.

**Potentially compatible (observe carefully):**
- Otocinclus catfish — Fast enough to avoid aggression; small enough not to be perceived as prey
- Corydoras (armoured; fins are harder targets) — **[See Corydoras care guide](/species/corydoras)**
- Fast-moving schooling fish with short fins (Ember Tetras, Chili Rasbora) — Short fins reduce nipping risk; speed helps avoidance
- Mystery snails (large ones — small snails will be eaten as food)

**Never keep with:**
- Long-finned fish of any species
- Slow-moving fish
- Shrimp (will be eaten)
- Other puffer species (interspecies aggression)

## Sexing Pea Puffers

**Males:** Develop a dark stripe running down the mid-belly when mature. More vivid yellow colouration. May develop "wrinkles" around the eyes (age-related).

**Females:** Rounded body shape when gravid (carrying eggs). Belly spot patterns more pronounced. Generally slightly larger body.

Sexing is reliable in adults (8+ months) but difficult in juveniles.

## Breeding

Pea Puffers have been successfully bred in captivity but it requires a dedicated breeding setup.

**Conditioning:** Feed live foods heavily — Daphnia, BBS, Bloodworm — for 2–3 weeks before attempting to breed.

**Setup:** Heavy plant coverage including Java Moss or fine-leaved plants for egg deposition. Slightly warmer water (27–28°C).

**Spawning:** The male courts the female with colour intensification and fin displays. Spawning occurs in or near plants — the male pursues the female and fertilises eggs as they are scattered among plant stems and moss. Eggs are small, transparent, and adhesive.

**Egg care:** Parents do not guard eggs. Remove adults after spawning or eggs will be consumed. Eggs hatch in 5–7 days at 27°C.

**Fry:** Tiny fry require infusoria and vinegar eels for first week, then **[Microworms](/tools)** and **[Baby Brine Shrimp](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods)** from week 2.

---

## Health

**Ich:** Common, particularly after transport stress. Heat treatment (30°C for 2 weeks) is safer than medication for scaleless fish. Monitor oxygenation carefully at elevated temperatures.

**External parasites (flukes):** Scratching behaviour, flicking against objects. Treat with Praziquantel at reduced doses.

**Internal parasites:** New fish commonly affected. Treat with Levamisole.

**Constipation:** Puffers fed exclusively bloodworm with no fibre (Daphnia, etc.) can become constipated. Fast for 24 hours, then feed live Daphnia.
