# Flame Angelfish: The Complete Dwarf Angel Reef Care Guide

The flame angelfish is one of the most spectacular fish in the marine hobby — a blazing red-orange dwarf angel barred in black with electric-blue-tipped fins, an active, bold, personable centerpiece for a reef. *Centropyge loriculus* is hardy and full of character, but like all dwarf angels it comes with a caveat reef keepers must weigh: it's "reef-safe with caution," meaning most individuals leave corals alone but some nip. Understanding its needs — space, an algae-forward diet, and that coral-nipping risk — is the key to keeping this fiery jewel well.

This guide is the complete reference: flame angel biology, the reef-safe-with-caution question, diet, tank size, and tank mates.

---

## Species Overview

The flame angelfish (*Centropyge loriculus*) is a dwarf angelfish reaching about 10 cm (4 inches). Its colour is its glory: a brilliant **red-to-orange body** crossed by several **black vertical bars**, with **electric-blue-and-black edging** on the rear fins — one of the most vivid, eye-catching fish in the hobby. It's an active, bold dwarf angel that grazes the rockwork and makes a stunning centerpiece.

The flame angel is **hardy, active, personable, and beautiful**, but it's rated intermediate for two reasons: it needs **space and a mature, algae-rich tank**, and — like all *Centropyge* dwarf angels — it's **"reef-safe with caution,"** meaning while most individuals don't bother corals, some will nip LPS coral polyps or clam mantles, so it carries a coral-nipping risk in a mixed reef. It's an omnivore that grazes algae and detritus, needing an algae-forward diet. With good care it lives 5–7 years. For a keeper who can provide the space and accept the (usually low) coral risk, the flame angel is one of the most spectacular reef centerpieces available; aquacultured specimens are increasingly available and hardy.

## Natural History and Origin

*Centropyge loriculus* is found across the **Pacific Ocean** on coral-rich reef slopes, where it darts among coral and rubble grazing **algae, detritus, and biofilm** (aufwuchs), staying close to cover. As a dwarf angel it's an active grazer that ranges over the reef, picking at surfaces all day, and it shelters in crevices when threatened.

Its grazing, omnivorous lifestyle defines its care: it needs a **mature, algae-rich tank** with plenty of rockwork to graze and hide in, and an **algae-forward diet** in captivity. The "reef-safe with caution" behaviour stems from this grazing nature — a dwarf angel that picks at surfaces all day may sample coral polyps or clam mantles, especially if underfed or in a sparse tank; a well-fed angel in a mature, algae-rich reef is least likely to bother corals. As a protogynous hermaphrodite (like many angels), dominant individuals can change sex, but home breeding is not practical. Its activity, grazing, and coral-nipping tendency all stem from this reef-grazing natural history.

Flame angels need stable, **mature** marine conditions — a mature reef with established algae growth is important (both for their grazing diet and their settling in). Mix salt with RO/DI water, target salinity with a refractometer, and add them only to a fully cycled, mature tank — confirm with the [nitrogen cycle tracker](/tools/nitrogen-cycle) and the [water parameters reference](/tools/water-parameters). Stability and good water quality keep them healthy and colourful.

## Tank Setup Guide

### Tank size

A flame angel needs a **minimum of 55 gallons (210 litres)**, with more better — they're active grazers that range over the reef and need room and plenty of rockwork to graze and feel secure. A small or sparse tank stresses them and increases the chance of coral-nipping (a bored, underfed angel is more likely to sample corals).

### Aquascape — mature and algae-rich

Provide **abundant live rock** with caves and grazing surfaces, in a **mature tank with established algae and biofilm** for the angel to graze. This rockwork gives it food, hiding spots, and territory. A mature, algae-rich reef is ideal — it satisfies the angel's grazing and reduces coral interest.

### Filtration, flow, lid

Run a standard reef system with good flow and a protein skimmer. A lid is sensible. The priority is a **mature, well-established, algae-rich reef** with ample rockwork.

## Feeding Guide — Algae-Forward

Flame angels are omnivores that need an **algae-forward diet** — proper feeding is also the best insurance against coral-nipping.

### What to feed

- **Marine algae (nori) and algae-based foods** — the cornerstone; clip nori and offer angel/spirulina-based foods.
- **Mysis and enriched brine shrimp** — meaty foods for balance.
- **Sponge-based "angelfish formula" foods** — dwarf angels benefit from sponge matter in the diet.
- **Natural algae and biofilm** grazed from the mature rockwork.
- **Vitamin-soaked foods** — help prevent disease and keep colour vivid.

### How often

Feed **two to three times daily**, algae-forward and varied. A **well-fed flame angel is far less likely to nip corals** — keeping it satisfied with algae and prepared foods is the practical key to "reef-safe with caution." A healthy flame angel is plump, vividly coloured, and grazing actively.

## Behaviour, Reef Safety and Tank Mates

Flame angels are **active, bold, and personable** — they graze the rockwork, dart among coral, and make an engaging, fiery centerpiece. The central consideration is **reef safety**: they're **"reef-safe with caution,"** meaning most individuals leave corals alone, but **some nip LPS coral polyps and clam mantles**, and it's individual-dependent and unpredictable. To minimise risk: keep the angel **well-fed (algae-forward)** in a **mature, algae-rich tank** with **plenty of grazing**, and watch new additions around prized corals. Many keepers run flame angels in mixed reefs successfully; others find an individual that nips. It's a calculated risk.

**Other dwarf angels:** keep **only one dwarf angel per tank** (they're territorial toward each other) unless the tank is very large. **Tank mates:** flame angels suit robust reef communities — [percula clownfish](/species/percula-clownfish), [yellow tang](/species/yellow-tang), [six-line wrasse](/species/six-line-wrasse), [banggai cardinalfish](/species/banggai-cardinalfish), and other peaceful-to-semi-aggressive reef fish. Avoid very aggressive fish and other dwarf angels. Use the [compatibility checker](/tools/fish-compatibility).

## Breeding Guide

Flame angels are **not bred in home aquaria** — like other marine angels they're pelagic spawners with tiny, difficult-to-rear larvae, requiring specialised larval systems beyond practical home setups (though aquaculture of dwarf angels has had some commercial breakthroughs, increasing captive-bred availability). They're protogynous hermaphrodites (females can become males), and in the wild a dominant male maintains a harem, but home breeding isn't achievable for hobbyists.

For keepers, the flame angel is a spectacular display fish to enjoy. Choosing aquacultured specimens where available supports the hobby and reduces wild collection.

## Health and Disease

Flame angels are hardy when well-fed in a mature tank, with the usual marine concerns.

**Marine ich (*Cryptocaryon*)** and **velvet (*Amyloodinium*)** can affect them — treat in quarantine (angels can be sensitive to copper, so research dosing and consider alternatives). **Head and lateral line erosion (HLLE)** is linked to poor diet (insufficient algae/vitamins) and poor water — prevent with an algae-forward, vitamin-rich diet and good water quality. **Bacterial infections** follow poor water or stress. A mature, algae-rich tank and good feeding are central to their health (and to reef safety).

Prevention: **quarantine new fish**, keep parameters stable in a **mature tank**, feed an **algae-forward, vitamin-rich diet**, and provide ample rockwork. Given those, the flame angel is a hardy, vivid, long-lived reef centerpiece.
