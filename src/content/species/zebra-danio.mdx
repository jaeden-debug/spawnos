# Zebra Danio: The Complete Care, Schooling & Breeding Guide

The zebra danio is one of the hardiest, most active, and most beginner-friendly fish in the entire hobby — a tireless little striped torpedo that zips around the upper tank in busy schools, tolerates a wide range of conditions (including cool water), and breeds so easily it's a classic first spawning project. *Danio rerio* (the "zebrafish") is also the most important fish in science. Bombproof, cheap, peaceful, and endlessly energetic, it's the definitive starter schooling fish — provided you give it a group and swimming room.

This guide is the complete reference: zebra danio biology, the cool-tolerant care it needs, why it must be kept in a group, easy breeding, the science story, and tank mates.

---

## Species Overview

The zebra danio (*Danio rerio*) is a small cyprinid from South Asia reaching about 5 cm (2 inches). It's named for the **horizontal blue-and-gold "zebra" stripes** running the length of its slim, torpedo-shaped body. Selective breeding has produced **long-fin** and **leopard** (spotted) variants, plus the controversial fluorescent **"GloFish"** (genetically-modified colour forms). It's a fast, constantly-moving fish that occupies the upper water column.

The zebra danio is **extraordinarily hardy, peaceful, active, and beginner-friendly** — arguably the toughest common aquarium fish, tolerating a wide range of temperature (it's notably **cool-tolerant**), pH, and hardness. It's a **schooling fish** that's active and confident in a group and nippy/stressed in small numbers. It breeds with almost no effort, making it a classic first spawning project, and it's the world's premier **scientific model organism** (the "zebrafish"). With good care it lives 3–5 years. For a first fish, a cool-water tank, or an active dither species, the zebra danio is hard to beat.

## Natural History and Origin

*Danio rerio* is native to the streams, rivers, rice paddies, and seasonal pools of **South Asia** (India, Bangladesh, Nepal, and surrounding areas), spanning a wide range of conditions from cool hill streams to warmer lowland waters — which is exactly why it's so adaptable and cool-tolerant in the aquarium. It's a **fast-swimming, schooling fish** of flowing and still waters, feeding on insects, small invertebrates, and other small foods near the surface.

This adaptable, wide-ranging, schooling natural history underpins its legendary hardiness and its needs: a **group**, **swimming room**, and **some flow/oxygen**. As an egg-scattering fish that breeds readily across a range of conditions, it's a classic easy breeder. And famously, *Danio rerio* became the **dominant vertebrate model organism in science** — its transparent embryos, rapid development, prolific breeding, and genetic tractability have made it central to research in genetics, development, medicine, and toxicology. Its hardiness, schooling, easy breeding, and scientific importance all stem from this adaptable, fast-living, wide-ranging biology.

Zebra danios are **exceptionally hardy and adaptable**, tolerating a wide range of temperature (including cool, unheated conditions), pH, and hardness — which is why they're a classic beginner fish and were long (mis)used to "cycle" tanks (a practice now discouraged for fish welfare; use a fishless cycle instead — see the [nitrogen cycle tracker](/tools/nitrogen-cycle)). They appreciate clean, well-oxygenated water with some flow, reflecting their stream origins. Their cool tolerance makes them great for unheated/temperate tanks and a poor long-term match for warm-water-only setups. Check values with the [water parameters reference](/tools/water-parameters).

## Tank Setup Guide

### Tank size

A school of zebra danios needs a **minimum of 15 gallons (57 litres)**, with 20+ gallons better — they're small but **extremely active swimmers** that need horizontal swimming room, so a longer tank is ideal. Always keep a **group of at least 6**, ideally more.

### Aquascape — swimming room and flow

Provide **open swimming space** (their priority) framed by planting and some décor. They appreciate **some flow and good oxygenation** (reflecting their stream origins) — they'll often play in a gentle current. A planted tank with open upper water suits them; they're not fussy about aquascape. Cool, clean, oxygenated water with room to swim is the key.

### Filtration, flow, lid

Use reliable filtration with **moderate flow** and good oxygenation. A **lid is recommended** — active danios are jumpers. Their hardiness makes them forgiving of standard setups, as long as they have a group and swimming room.

## Feeding Guide

Zebra danios are omnivores that feed eagerly near the surface.

### What to feed

- **Quality flake and micro-pellets** — a convenient staple (they feed at the top).
- **Live and frozen [daphnia](/species/daphnia) and [baby brine shrimp](/species/baby-brine-shrimp)** — relished, excellent for colour and conditioning.
- Some **vegetable/spirulina** content for balance.

### How often

Feed **small amounts two to three times daily**. Zebra danios are fast, enthusiastic feeders (no risk of being outcompeted — they're often the ones outcompeting others). A varied diet keeps them in good condition and conditions them for breeding. A healthy zebra danio is active, well-striped, and constantly on the move. Avoid overfeeding their eager appetites.

## Behaviour and Tank Mates

Zebra danios are **peaceful, hardy, and relentlessly active** — they zip around the upper tank in a busy school, full of energy. They're a **schooling fish** that must be kept in a **group of 6 or more**: in a proper school they direct their energy and any chasing within the group, but in small numbers they can become nippy toward tank mates (and stressed themselves). Their constant activity makes them excellent **dither fish** that bring shyer species into the open.

Toward other species they're peaceful, though their **speed and energy** can stress slow, shy, or long-finned tank mates (and a too-small group of danios may nip fins). **Good tank mates** are other active, similarly-sized fish — [white cloud mountain minnows](/species/white-cloud-minnow), [harlequin rasboras](/species/harlequin-rasbora), [corydoras](/species/corydoras), [cherry barbs](/species/cherry-barb), [tiger barbs](/species/tiger-barb), and cool-tolerant community fish. Avoid slow long-finned fish (like fancy [bettas](/species/betta-fish) or [guppies](/species/guppy)) that the danios' energy may stress, and warm-water-only species. ## Breeding Guide — Easy Egg-Scatterers

Zebra danios are **one of the easiest fish to breed** — a classic first egg-scattering project. **Sexing**: females are rounder and fuller-bodied (especially when full of eggs); males are slimmer and often more intensely coloured/golden.

They're **egg-scatterers** that **eagerly eat their own eggs**, so the technique is about protecting the eggs. Condition a group on rich foods, then set up a breeding tank with a **layer of marbles, a mesh, or dense fine-leaved plants/[Java moss](/species/java-moss)** on the bottom so scattered eggs fall out of reach of the parents. Danios typically spawn in the morning, scattering eggs over the substrate/plants; **remove the adults afterward**. Eggs hatch in 2–3 days, and the fry become free-swimming a few days later, reared on [infusoria](/species/infusoria) and then microworms and [baby brine shrimp](/species/baby-brine-shrimp). They grow quickly and are hardy. Their reliable, easy spawning is exactly why they're the standard introduction to breeding egg-scatterers (and why they're so prolific in science).

## Health and Disease

Zebra danios are among the hardiest aquarium fish, and disease is uncommon with basic care.

**Ich** can follow temperature swings or stress; treat promptly. **Bacterial and fungal infections, fin issues** follow poor water or — in too-small a group — nipping injuries. **Stress** from being kept singly/in small numbers manifests as nippiness and reduced wellbeing. Their cool tolerance gives flexibility, but sustained high heat shortens their lives. Otherwise they're remarkably tough.

Prevention: a **stable, cycled, well-oxygenated tank** with **cool-to-moderate water**, a **group of 6+**, some flow, a varied diet, a lid against jumping, and quarantine of new arrivals. Given those basics, zebra danios are about as bulletproof and forgiving as aquarium fish get — which is why they're the classic beginner and model-organism fish.

## Interesting Facts

- **The most important fish in science.** *Danio rerio* (the zebrafish) is the premier vertebrate model organism, central to genetics, development, and medical research thanks to its transparent embryos and prolific breeding.
- **Bombproof and cool-tolerant.** Among the hardiest aquarium fish, it thrives across a wide range and tolerates cool, unheated tanks.
- **Energetic schoolers.** Relentlessly active, they need a group and swimming room — and direct their energy within the school.
- **Easy egg-scatterers.** They breed with almost no effort (the trick is protecting the eggs from the parents), the classic first spawning project.
- **GloFish are zebra danios.** The fluorescent "GloFish" are genetically-modified zebra danios — the same hardy fish in engineered colours.

## Bringing It Together

The zebra danio is the definitive hardy, active, beginner-friendly schooling fish — bombproof, cool-tolerant, peaceful, cheap, and so easy to breed it's a classic first spawning project (and the world's most important scientific fish). Give it a **15-gallon-plus tank with open swimming room**, **cool-to-moderate, clean, well-oxygenated water** (no heater needed in many homes), some flow, a varied diet, and a lid against jumping — and keep a **group of 6 or more** to channel its energy and prevent nippiness. Pair it with other active, cool-tolerant fish like the [white cloud mountain minnow](/species/white-cloud-minnow), [harlequin rasbora](/species/harlequin-rasbora), and [corydoras](/species/corydoras), avoiding slow long-finned tank mates. Forgiving, energetic, and endlessly active, it's an ideal first fish and a lively dither species for cool-water and community tanks alike.
