## Species Overview {#overview}

The **guppy** (*Poecilia reticulata*) is the world's most widely kept freshwater aquarium fish by sheer population count. Hardy, colorful, livebearing, and extraordinarily easy to breed, guppies have been fundamental to the aquarium hobby since the early 20th century and are often the first fish kept by new aquarists. Yet beneath the beginner-friendly exterior lies a species with remarkable biological complexity: guppies are one of the most studied model organisms in evolutionary biology, mate choice research, and predator-prey dynamics.

*Poecilia reticulata* belongs to the family Poeciliidae — the live-bearing toothcarps — a New World family containing mollies, platys, swordtails, and their relatives. All Poeciliidae share the trait of viviparous reproduction: females retain fertilized eggs internally and give birth to fully-formed, free-swimming young.

The species was first described by Peters in 1859 from Trinidad specimens, though it was independently described multiple times under different names (including *Girardinus guppii*, the origin of the common name "guppy," named after the naturalist Robert John Lechmere Guppy who sent specimens to the British Museum in 1866).

## Natural Habitat {#natural-habitat}

### Distribution and Wild Range

Wild guppies are native to northeastern South America and the Caribbean — specifically Trinidad and Tobago, Barbados, Antigua and Barbuda, and coastal Venezuela, Guyana, and northeastern Brazil. They have been introduced widely across the tropics and subtropics for mosquito control (like bettas, guppies are natural predators of mosquito larvae), establishing feral populations on every populated continent.

The wild habitat spans a remarkable ecological range: from mountain streams at altitude to lowland coastal rivers, estuaries, and even mildly brackish water. This ecological flexibility is a major reason for the guppy's success as both aquarium fish and introduced species.

### Water Chemistry

Wild guppy populations show great variation in water chemistry tolerance corresponding to their diverse habitats:

- **Temperature:** 18–28°C (64–82°F) — one of the widest thermal tolerances in ornamental fish
- **pH:** 6.5–8.5 — genuinely tolerant of a wide range
- **General Hardness (GH):** 8–20 dGH — wild populations are predominantly from hard-water environments; they are NOT soft-water fish
- **Carbonate Hardness (KH):** 6–15 dKH
- **TDS:** 150–500 ppm

This is critically different from Amazonian fish like neon tetras: guppies come from **hard, alkaline water** and thrive in it. The addition of tannins is less beneficial for guppies than for blackwater species, though the antimicrobial properties of tannins are universally applicable.

### Wild Diet

Wild guppies are omnivores, feeding primarily on:
- Algae and algal mats — surface grazing
- Mosquito larvae — a primary animal protein source
- Small aquatic invertebrates — copepods, Daphnia, small worms
- Detritus — organic particles in the water column
- Occasional terrestrial insects fallen on the surface

Their upturned mouths indicate a surface and mid-column feeding preference.

## Aquarium Care Requirements {#aquarium-care}

### Water Parameters

| Parameter | Optimal Range | Acceptable Range |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|
| Temperature | 22–26°C (72–79°F) | 18–30°C |
| pH | 7.0–8.0 | 6.5–8.5 |
| General Hardness (GH) | 10–20 dGH | 7–30 dGH |
| Carbonate Hardness (KH) | 6–12 dKH | 4–15 dKH |
| Ammonia | 0 ppm | — |
| Nitrite | 0 ppm | — |
| Nitrate | <20 ppm | <40 ppm |

Guppies **prefer hard, alkaline water**. Soft, acidic water (which beginner keepers sometimes use thinking it's universally beneficial) actually stresses guppies. If your tap water is very soft, add crushed coral to the filter to raise GH and KH.

> Use the [GH/KH Hardness Converter](/tools/gh-kh-converter) to verify your water hardness is in the appropriate range for guppies.

### Tank Size and Stocking

Minimum: 40 litres (10 US gallons) for a small group. Guppies are prolific breeders — a tank will rapidly become overstocked if both males and females are present. Common approaches:

- **Males-only tank** — avoids breeding, less stress on females, brilliant color display; minimum 5–6 males together (single males are often harassed by other males without an outlet)
- **Females-only tank** — females are less colorful but peaceful
- **Breeding colony** — males and females together with ratio of 1 male to 2–3 females to distribute harassment

## Tank Setup Guide {#tank-setup}

Guppies appreciate planted tanks with cover, open swimming space, and gentle to moderate flow.

**Substrate:** Medium-grain sand or fine gravel. Guppies are not substrate specialists — almost any clean substrate works.

**Plants:** Dense planting provides cover for fry and reduces aggression. Excellent choices: *Hygrophila*, *Ludwigia*, *Bacopa*, Java Fern, *Vallisneria*, hornwort, floating plants (water sprite, frogbit). Dense floating cover is particularly valuable — guppies spend considerable time near the surface.

**Filtration:** Moderate flow is acceptable; guppies are stronger swimmers than bettas. However, fry are extremely vulnerable to filter intake — use a pre-filter sponge on the intake if breeding.

**Hardscape:** Driftwood, smooth rocks, ceramic caves for hiding. Less critical than for more territorial species.

## Fin Types and Colour Morphs {#morphs}

The ornamental guppy hobby has developed an extraordinary diversity of color and fin forms through selective breeding, with formal competitions and breed standards maintained by guppy societies worldwide.

### Tail Types
- **Fan Tail / Delta:** Wide, triangular caudal fin; the most common ornamental form
- **Sword Tail:** Single elongated ray extending from the caudal fin — upper sword, lower sword, or double sword variants
- **Lyretail:** Both upper and lower lobes extended, with concave central section
- **Veil Tail:** Long, flowing, and rounded
- **Spear Tail:** Pointed, narrow caudal fin
- **Pin Tail / Needle Tail:** Extreme point; relatively rare
- **Moscow Blue / Moscow types:** Specific color breeds with near-solid body coloration, often paired with large delta tails

### Color Patterns
Guppy coloration is extraordinarily diverse and partially sex-linked. Males display vivid colors; females are typically grey-brown with modest iridescence (though some fancy strains have colored females). Color forms include:

- **Solid:** Single color covering body and fins
- **Tuxedo:** Dark black or blue body coloration from mid-body to tail, contrasting head
- **Cobra / Snakeskin:** Rosette or chain-link pattern on body
- **Moscow:** Near-solid body color with metallic iridescence
- **Mosaic:** Random color patchwork
- **Endler's livebearer** (*Poecilia wingei*): A closely related species sometimes crossed with guppies; smaller, more intense colors, often kept separately by purists

## Feeding Guide {#feeding}

Guppies are straightforward omnivores and accept virtually any aquarium food readily.

**Recommended feeding:**
- High-quality flake or micro-pellet as staple — look for protein-rich formulations with minimal filler
- Frozen foods: Daphnia, brine shrimp, bloodworms, cyclops — offered 2–3 times per week
- Live foods from [Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods):
  - **Live Daphnia** — natural food; enrichment and conditioning
  - **Live Baby Brine Shrimp** — the best conditioning food for show guppies and pre-breeding conditioning; dramatically improves color
  - **Microworms** — essential first food for guppy fry (though fry are larger than neon tetra fry and can accept BBS from birth if nauplii are fresh)

Feed small amounts 2–3 times daily. Guppies will readily overeat; maintain portion control to prevent water quality issues.

## Breeding Guide {#breeding}

### Livebearer Reproduction

Guppies are viviparous livebearers. The male possesses a modified anal fin called a **gonopodium** — a tube-shaped structure used to internally fertilize females. A single mating can fertilize multiple successive broods because females store sperm in specialized crypts in the ovary.

**Gestation period:** 21–35 days depending on temperature. At 25°C, approximately 28 days. At 22°C, closer to 35 days.

**Brood size:** 5–80 fry per birth, depending on female age, condition, and size. Older, larger females produce bigger broods.

**Fry at birth:** Fully formed miniature guppies, 3–6mm long, immediately free-swimming. They do not require special conditions and can accept food immediately.

### Fry Care and First Foods

Unlike egg-laying species, guppy fry are robust at birth and can accept relatively large foods. However, the best early growth and survival rates come from live foods:

- **Baby Brine Shrimp (BBS)** — freshly hatched nauplii are the ideal first food for guppy fry. Available from [Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods).
- **Microworms** — good supplemental first food if BBS is not available; appropriate size for newborns
- **Crushed flake** — will work in absence of live food but produces slower growth
- **Live Daphnia** — fry can begin taking small Daphnia within 1–2 weeks

The primary threat to fry in a community tank is predation by adult guppies and other fish. Dense planting and floating plants provide cover. Separate breeding boxes (submersed within the main tank) or a separate fry tank provide the best survival rates.

## Compatibility {#compatibility}

Guppies are peaceful community fish. Avoid:
- **Male bettas** — will shred guppy fins and mistake males for rival bettas
- **Large cichlids, oscars, angelfish in small tanks** — will eat guppies
- **Tiger barbs, serpae tetras, fin-nippers** — will destroy fancy guppy finnage

**Excellent companions:** [Corydoras](/species/corydoras), [neon tetras](/species/neon-tetra), platys, mollies, [cherry shrimp](/species/cherry-shrimp), [bristlenose plecos](/species/bristlenose-pleco), small peaceful tetras, [kuhli loaches](/species/kuhli-loach).

## Health and Disease {#health}

Guppies are generally hardy but susceptible to:

**Fin Rot:** Bacterial erosion of fin tissue; caused by poor water quality or physical damage. Improve water quality immediately; antibiotic treatment for severe cases.

**Ich:** White spot disease; standard temperature elevation and treatment applies.

**Wasting Disease / Internal Parasites:** Guppies commonly carry internal parasites (*Camallanus* worms, other nematodes) visible as red threads protruding from the anus. Treatment: fenbendazole or levamisole-based medications.

**Bent Spine (Guppy Disease):** A catch-all term for progressive spinal curvature. Causes include genetic defects (common in inbred fancy lines), *Mycobacterium* bacterial infection (untreatable), or environmental causes. Fish-in-a-family-tree inbreeding over many generations is the most common cause in fancy show guppy lines.
