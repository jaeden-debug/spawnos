# Royal Dottyback: The Complete Reef Care & Tank Mate Guide

The royal dottyback is a jewel with attitude — a tiny fish split sharply into electric purple and bright yellow, hardy and reef-safe and useful for controlling bristleworms, but pugnacious far beyond its size. *Pictichromis paccagnellae* packs the colour of a much pricier fish into a cheap, tough package, but its bold temperament means it needs careful stocking, and it's frequently confused with the gentler [royal gramma](/species/royal-gramma) — a confusion that causes real tank conflict.

This guide is the complete reference: royal dottyback biology, managing its aggression, its pest-control value, the gramma look-alike issue, and tank mates.

---

## Species Overview

The royal dottyback (*Pictichromis paccagnellae*), also called the bicolor or false gramma dottyback, is a small marine fish reaching about 7 cm (3 inches). Its colour is striking and unmistakable: the **front half electric purple**, the **back half bright yellow**, meeting at a **crisp, hard dividing line** (the key feature distinguishing it from the gradual-blending [royal gramma](/species/royal-gramma)).

The royal dottyback is **hardy, reef-safe, colourful, and inexpensive** — and helps control reef pests like bristleworms — but it's **bold and aggressive for its size**, harassing shy, slow, or similar-looking fish, which is the defining husbandry consideration. It's rated beginner-friendly on care, with the challenge being its temperament and stocking. With good care it lives 5–7 years. For a reef with robust tank mates (and a keeper who stocks the dottyback thoughtfully), it's a tough, vivid, characterful jewel — and a useful pest-hunter. Note there are several dottyback species (orchid, neon, etc.) with varying temperaments; the royal dottyback is one of the feistier ones.

## Natural History and Origin

*Pictichromis paccagnellae* ranges across the **Western Pacific**, living on coral-rich reef slopes where it hides in caves and rubble and darts out to feed on zooplankton and small invertebrates. It's a secretive, territorial fish that claims a crevice and defends a patch of reef, hunting micro-prey and bristleworms from cover.

This cave-dwelling, territorial, hunting lifestyle is the basis of both its value and its temperament. Its appetite for **bristleworms** and small invertebrates makes it a useful biological pest controller, and its hardiness reflects an adaptable, opportunistic nature. But its **territoriality and boldness** — natural traits for defending a reef crevice — translate into aggression toward tank mates, especially shy, slow, or similar-coloured fish, in the confines of an aquarium. Many dottybacks are also protogynous hermaphrodites. Its colour, hardiness, pest-hunting, and feistiness all stem from this territorial, cave-dwelling reef life.

## Tank Setup Guide

### Tank size

A royal dottyback is comfortable in a **30-gallon (115-litre)** reef, with more space helping to dilute its aggression toward tank mates. While small, its feistiness means a too-small or sparse tank concentrates conflict.

### Aquascape — caves and rock

Provide **plenty of live rock with caves and crevices** — the dottyback claims and defends a cave, hunts through the rock for pests, and shelters there. Abundant rockwork is essential both for its natural behaviour and for **managing aggression** (broken sightlines and territory let other fish stay clear). A mature reef with lots of structure suits it.

### Filtration, flow, lid

Run a standard reef system. A lid is sensible (dottybacks can jump). A rock-rich reef gives the dottyback territory and gives tank mates refuge from its assertiveness.

## Feeding Guide

Royal dottybacks are carnivores that hunt small prey and readily take prepared foods.

### What to feed

- **Frozen mysis and enriched brine shrimp** — readily accepted staples.
- **Marine pellets/flake** — most dottybacks take prepared foods eagerly.
- **Reef pests** — bristleworms and small invertebrates it hunts naturally.
- **[Copepods](/species/copepods)** and small live foods — relished.

### How often

Feed **once or twice daily**. They're eager, bold feeders (no risk of being outcompeted — quite the opposite). A varied diet keeps the vivid purple-and-yellow colour saturated. A healthy royal dottyback is bold, brightly coloured, and active around its cave.

## Behaviour, Aggression and the Gramma Look-Alike

The royal dottyback is **bold, hardy, reef-safe, and a useful pest-hunter — but feisty and territorial for its size**, which is the defining husbandry consideration. As it settles, it claims a cave and **harasses shy, slow, or similar-looking fish**, and it can bully tank mates well beyond its small size. This makes it "reef-safe" (no harm to corals/inverts) but potentially a problem for peaceful fish.

**Management:** **add the dottyback last** (so it doesn't establish dominance first), provide **ample rockwork** to break sightlines, choose **robust tank mates** that can hold their own, and **avoid shy/slow fish** (like [firefish](/species/firefish-goby) or [mandarins](/species/mandarin-dragonet)) in smaller tanks. Critically, **do not keep it with the [royal gramma](/species/royal-gramma)** — they look similar (purple-and-yellow), see each other as rivals, and the more aggressive dottyback will harass the gramma; choose one or the other (the gramma's colours blend gradually, the dottyback's meet at a hard line). **Good tank mates** include [percula clownfish](/species/percula-clownfish), [yellow tang](/species/yellow-tang), [foxface rabbitfish](/species/foxface-rabbitfish), and robust reef fish. Keep only one dottyback per tank. Use the [compatibility checker](/tools/fish-compatibility).

## Breeding Guide

Royal dottybacks (and dottybacks generally) are among the **more achievable marine fish to breed** — they're cave/crevice spawners where the male guards a clutch of eggs in a cave, and some species (like the orchid dottyback) are commercially captive-bred. Home breeding is possible for the dedicated keeper: a bonded pair spawns in a cave, the male tends the eggs, and the larvae (smaller and more demanding than [Banggai cardinalfish](/species/banggai-cardinalfish) fry) are reared on [rotifers](/species/rotifers) then [baby brine shrimp](/species/baby-brine-shrimp).

It's more involved than mouthbrooding cardinalfish but more achievable than many marine fish, making dottybacks a reasonable intermediate marine breeding project. For most keepers, the royal dottyback is a hardy, colourful display fish; choosing captive-bred dottybacks (where available) supports the hobby.

## Health and Disease

Royal dottybacks are hardy, with the usual marine concerns reduced by quarantine and stability.

**Marine ich (*Cryptocaryon*)** and **velvet (*Amyloodinium*)** can affect them — treat in quarantine with appropriate therapy. **Bacterial infections** follow poor water or injury. **Jumping** is a risk — keep a lid. Otherwise they're robust; their boldness and bright colour are signs of good health.

Prevention: **quarantine new fish**, keep parameters stable, feed a varied diet, provide ample rockwork, use a lid, and stock thoughtfully around their aggression. Given those, the royal dottyback is a hardy, colourful, long-lived reef fish.

## Interesting Facts

- **A jewel with attitude.** Its crisp purple-and-yellow split makes it look like a far pricier fish, but it's cheap, tough, and feisty.
- **Not the royal gramma.** The dottyback's colours meet at a hard line and it's aggressive; the [royal gramma](/species/royal-gramma)'s blend gradually and it's peaceful — never keep the two together.
- **A pest-hunter.** It controls bristleworms and small invertebrates, a useful reef service.
- **Bold beyond its size.** Feisty and territorial, it can harass much larger or peaceful fish — stock it last and with robust tank mates.
- **Breedable.** Dottybacks are among the more achievable marine fish to breed (some are commercially captive-bred).

## Bringing It Together

The royal dottyback is a vivid, hardy, inexpensive reef jewel that earns its place hunting bristleworms and adding bold colour — but its feisty, territorial temperament defines how you stock it. Give it a **30-gallon-plus reef with plenty of caves and rockwork**, stable conditions, and a varied diet, and manage its aggression by **adding it last**, choosing **robust tank mates**, avoiding shy/slow fish, and keeping only one per tank. Above all, **never keep it with the look-alike [royal gramma](/species/royal-gramma)** — they fight as rivals (remember: dottyback = hard colour line and aggressive; gramma = gradual blend and peaceful). It pairs well with sturdy reef fish like the [yellow tang](/species/yellow-tang), [foxface rabbitfish](/species/foxface-rabbitfish), and [percula clownfish](/species/percula-clownfish), and is even a reasonable marine breeding project.
