## Species Overview {#overview}

The **freshwater angelfish** (*Pterophyllum scalare*) is one of the most graceful and recognizable aquarium fish in the world. Its laterally compressed, disc-shaped body, dramatically elongated dorsal and anal fins, and distinctive vertical banding pattern give it a silhouette unlike any other common aquarium fish. First imported to Europe in the early 1900s and to North America by the 1920s, angelfish have been bred in captivity for over a century, producing dozens of color varieties while retaining their essential wild character.

Despite their widespread availability, angelfish are regularly misunderstood and poorly kept. They are South American cichlids with specific water chemistry preferences, significant adult size, territorial temperament, and social complexity. Understanding their natural biology transforms angelfish keeping from managing a display fish to engaging with a genuinely intelligent, behaviorally rich species.

### Scientific Classification

| Rank | Classification |

The genus *Pterophyllum* contains three species: *P. scalare* (the common aquarium angelfish), *P. altum* (the altum angelfish — deeper bodied, more demanding, commands extreme soft acidic water), and *P. leopoldi* (the smallest species, rarely kept). All three are Amazonian cichlids; *P. altum* is the blackwater extreme specialist of the three, inhabiting the Rio Orinoco and upper Rio Negro systems at pH values as low as 4.5.

*Pterophyllum scalare* was formally described by Lichtenstein in 1823 from specimens collected along the Amazon River. The genus name means "winged leaf" — a reference to the distinctive wing-like finnage profile of the fish.

## Natural Habitat {#natural-habitat}

### The Amazon River System

Wild *P. scalare* is distributed across a broad range in the Amazon basin, including the Amazon River mainstream and its major tributaries — the Rio Solimões, Rio Negro, Rio Tapajós, Rio Tocantins, and others. Within this vast range, angelfish occupy specific microhabitats: slow-moving to still water areas with abundant vertical structure.

Their preferred habitat consists of:
- **Flooded forest margins** (*igapó* and *várzea* flooded forests during high water season)
- **Margins of slow blackwater rivers** with dense root systems and fallen timber
- **Oxbow lakes** and isolated pools
- **Dense stands of tall aquatic plants** (*Echinodorus*, *Vallisneria*, tall sedges) in relatively shallow water

The vertical, elongated body shape of angelfish is a direct adaptation to this habitat — they are masters of hiding in plain sight among vertical plant stems and roots, appearing to be just another leaf or stem in the vegetation.

### Water Chemistry in the Wild

Wild *P. scalare* habitat spans a range of water chemistry conditions depending on geographic location:

- **Temperature:** 24–30°C (75–86°F) — warm tropical water
- **pH:** 5.5–7.5 — variable by location; blackwater tributaries (Rio Negro drainage) produce very acidic conditions; whitewater river margins are closer to neutral
- **General Hardness (GH):** 1–8 dGH — soft to very soft in most natural habitats
- **Carbonate Hardness (KH):** 1–4 dKH — low buffering capacity
- **TDS:** 10–150 ppm — relatively low mineral content

Wild angelfish are **soft-water, warm-water fish**. The hard, alkaline tap water common in many cities is suboptimal for angelfish health and reproduction, though commercial strains have been selectively adapted to tolerate harder water over generations of captive breeding.

### Blackwater Connection

Many of the most significant angelfish collecting sites are blackwater or blackwater-influenced environments. The Rio Negro — the world's largest blackwater river by volume — is home to angelfish populations living in water with pH as low as 4.5, GH essentially 0, and heavy tannin staining. While commercial *P. scalare* can be kept in harder water than wild fish, replicating blackwater conditions brings out their best color, reduces disease incidence, and promotes natural breeding behavior.

## Aquarium Care Requirements {#aquarium-care}

### Tank Dimensions: Height Matters

Angelfish are tall fish. An adult *P. scalare* can reach 15 cm body height from dorsal to anal fin tip — and with elongated finnage, the full vertical profile is even greater. A standard 40cm-deep tank is barely adequate; **60cm depth is the practical minimum** for adult angelfish.

Minimum tank: **120 litres (30 US gallons)** for a single pair or small group, with 60cm height. For a proper group of 6+, 300+ litres (80 gallons) is appropriate.

### Water Parameters

| Parameter | Optimal Range | Acceptable Range | Avoid |
|-----------|--------------|-----------------|-------|
| Temperature | 26–30°C (79–86°F) | 24–32°C | <22°C or >34°C |
| pH | 6.0–7.5 | 5.5–8.0 | >8.5 |
| General Hardness (GH) | 3–8 dGH | 2–15 dGH | >20 dGH |
| Carbonate Hardness (KH) | 2–5 dKH | 1–8 dKH | — |
| Ammonia | 0 ppm | — | >0.1 ppm |
| Nitrite | 0 ppm | — | >0.25 ppm |
| Nitrate | <20 ppm | <30 ppm | >50 ppm |

Angelfish are **sensitive to water quality changes**. Sudden parameter shifts trigger stress responses — sudden temperature drops, pH crashes, or spikes in ammonia cause visible behavioral changes (fin clamping, hiding, loss of color) within hours.

> Use the [Nitrogen Cycle Tracker](/tools/nitrogen-cycle) to fully establish the nitrogen cycle before adding angelfish. Unlike some hardier species, angelfish tolerate ammonia and nitrite poorly.

## Tank Setup Guide {#tank-setup}

### Replicating the Amazon Margin

The ideal angelfish tank replicates the vertical structure and soft, warm chemistry of flooded Amazonian forest margins.

**Height and vertical structure:** Tall plants are essential — *Vallisneria gigantea*, *Echinodorus bleheri* (Amazon sword), *Sagittaria subulata*, tall *Hygrophila* spp. Angelfish use vertical plant stems as hiding places, territory boundaries, and spawning sites. A tank without tall plants is a stressful, bare environment for these animals.

**Substrate:** Dark fine sand — best reflects their natural silty substrate and makes colors appear vivid. Nutrient-rich planted substrate benefits the tall, rooted plants angelfish thrive with.

**Filtration:** Moderate flow. Angelfish prefer gentle, low-current conditions — their tall finnage and disc-shaped body is not built for strong flow. A canister filter with a spray bar or output diffuser is ideal. Keep turnover at 3–5x tank volume per hour.

**Lighting:** Moderate. Strong lighting inhibits natural behavior; dimmer conditions (8–10 hours of moderate intensity) with floating plants for dappled coverage is ideal. Low-light and medium-light plants suit this approach.

**Botanicals and tannins:** Indian almond leaves, driftwood, and Amazonian botanicals create conditions that improve angelfish immune function, bring out color, and trigger breeding behavior. The amber-tinted water of a well-setup angelfish blackwater tank is visually extraordinary.

**Hardscape:** Driftwood pieces arranged vertically (branches, spider wood) replicate submerged tree root structure. Smooth rocks for territory marking. Avoid sharp or abrasive surfaces — angelfish trailing finnage is easily torn.

## Behaviour and Temperament {#behavior}

### Social Structure

Angelfish are cichlids — and like most cichlids, they are intelligent, territorial, and socially complex. They do not behave like peaceful community tetras; they have personalities, establish dominance hierarchies, form pair bonds, and defend territories.

In a group of 6+ juveniles, a dominance hierarchy develops as the fish mature. The dominant pair typically claims the best territory (usually near the best spawning site) and may harass subordinates. This is normal but must be managed: the tank must be large enough for subordinates to escape, and the dominant pair must be watched for excessive persecution of specific individuals.

**Pair bonding:** Once an angelfish pair forms, the bond is typically strong and lasting. Bonded pairs display synchronized swimming, mutual fin-raising, and cooperative territory defense. Separating a bonded pair causes visible distress in both fish.

### Aggression Toward Tank Mates

Angelfish aggression is primarily directed at:
1. **Other angelfish** — territory defense, especially in small tanks
2. **Small fish that fit in their mouths** — neon tetras are classic angelfish prey; a fish small enough to swallow will eventually be swallowed
3. **Fish that harass their fins** — barbs, danios, and other fin-nippers will shred angelfish finnage

Angelfish are **not appropriate** for nano community tanks with small tetras, unless the angelfish are juvenile (under 5cm body diameter) and the tank is very large and heavily planted.

## Compatibility {#compatibility}

**Compatible tank mates for angelfish:**
- **[Corydoras catfish](/species/corydoras)** — peaceful bottom dwellers too armored to be harassed; occupy different water column layer. A classic and reliable pairing.
- **Large tetras** — rummy nose tetras, black skirt tetras, bleeding heart tetras — large enough not to be eaten, peaceful enough not to harass
- **[Bristlenose plecos](/species/bristlenose-pleco)** — algae-eating, armored, peaceful; compatible at all angelfish sizes
- **Discus** — similar water requirements; stunning display combination; requires pristine water and experienced care
- **Rams (German Blue Ram, Bolivian Ram)** — dwarf cichlids compatible with angelfish; both Amazonian; requires adequate space
- **[Kuhli loaches](/species/kuhli-loach)** — peaceful nocturnal bottom fish; ignored by angelfish

**Incompatible:**
- [Neon tetras](/species/neon-tetra) (will be eaten by adult angelfish)
- Tiger barbs, serpae tetras (fin-nippers)
- Aggressive cichlids (jack dempseys, convicts)
- [Goldfish](/species/goldfish) (temperature and pH incompatibility)

## Feeding Guide {#feeding}

Angelfish are omnivores with a significant carnivorous lean. They are mid-column and surface feeders, using their upturned mouths to strike at prey from below.

**Recommended staple foods:**
- High-quality cichlid pellets (floating or slowly sinking, sized appropriately — ~2mm)
- Quality flake foods as a varied component
- Frozen foods: bloodworms, brine shrimp, mysis shrimp, beef heart (in moderation)

**Live foods from [Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods):**
- **Live Daphnia** — excellent conditioning food; stimulates natural hunting behavior; digestive health benefits
- **Live Baby Brine Shrimp** — the best conditioning food for pre-breeding angelfish and for raising angelfish fry
- **Live Scuds** — excellent enrichment food for adult angelfish

Condition breeding pairs with [live foods](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods) for 2–4 weeks before expected spawning. The improvement in spawn quality and fry survival rates from well-conditioned parents is dramatic.

## Breeding Guide {#breeding}

### Natural Pair Formation

Angelfish are biparental substrate spawners. They form monogamous pair bonds, jointly select and prepare a spawning site, spawn together, guard the eggs as a pair, and co-parent the fry. This is one of the most elaborate parental behaviors in freshwater fishkeeping.

The most reliable way to get a breeding pair is to raise a group of 6–8 juveniles together and allow natural pair formation. As the group matures, pairs will form organically. Once a pair has formed and begun defending territory, other fish should ideally be removed or the pair moved to a dedicated breeding tank.

**Sexing angelfish** is notoriously difficult until they are near breeding condition. Males typically develop a small nuchal hump (a raised area on the forehead above the eyes) and have a slightly more pointed breeding tube (papilla) which becomes visible when both fish are in breeding condition. The most reliable method is allowing the fish to pair themselves — a pair displaying mutual interest, swimming synchronized, and jointly defending territory is almost certainly male-female.

### Spawning Setup

A breeding tank for angelfish: 120–200 litres, tall (60cm+), bare or fine sand substrate, several vertical spawning sites (broad-leaved plants like *Echinodorus*, flat slate pieces leaned vertically, PVC pipe pieces), soft slightly acidic water (pH 6.5–7.0, GH 3–6), temperature 27–29°C, gentle filtration (sponge filter or baffled canister).

The pair will select a spawning surface, clean it obsessively for several days, then spawn across it in a sequence of passes — the female laying adhesive eggs in rows, the male following to fertilize them. A full clutch is typically 200–700+ eggs on a healthy adult pair.

### Egg and Fry Care

**Eggs:** Hatch in 36–48 hours at 28°C. The parents move the wrigglers (larvae) to pre-cleaned pits in the substrate or to plant leaves, continuing to fan them with their fins. Free-swimming fry emerge at 5–7 days.

**Parent-raised vs. artificially raised:** First-time spawning pairs frequently eat their eggs — this is normal. Many pairs require several spawning attempts before successful parenting instincts kick in. Once a pair successfully raises a batch, subsequent spawns are usually reliable.

For artificially raising eggs: remove the spawning slate/leaf, incubate eggs in a shallow container of tank water with gentle aeration and methylene blue (antifungal), hatch them separately, and begin feeding fry once free-swimming.

**Fry first foods:**
- **Newly free-swimming:** [Baby brine shrimp nauplii](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods) — the single best angelfish fry food. BBS nauplii are the perfect size for newly free-swimming angelfish fry and provide exceptional nutrition.
- **Week 2+:** [Microworms](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods) as supplemental food alongside BBS
- **Week 3+:** [Live Daphnia](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods) (small), micro pellets, finely crushed flake

Quality [live foods from Blackwater Aquatics](https://blackwateraquatics.ca/collections/live-foods) make the difference between mediocre and exceptional angelfish fry survival and growth rates.

## Health and Disease {#health}

**Hole in the Head (HITH) / Head and Lateral Line Erosion (HLLE):** Pitting and erosion around the head and along the lateral line. Associated with poor water quality, activated carbon overuse, and possibly hexamita parasite. Improve water quality, remove activated carbon, increase water change frequency, and consider metronidazole treatment for suspected hexamita involvement.

**Ich:** Standard treatment applies. Raise temperature to 29–30°C, treat with appropriate medication. Angelfish are somewhat sensitive to copper-based treatments — use half the recommended dose and monitor closely.

**Fin Rot:** Bacterial erosion from poor water quality. Improve conditions; antibiotics for severe cases.

**Internal Parasites (Hexamita, Spironucleus):** White, stringy feces; hollow belly; loss of appetite. Common in angelfish, especially those from poor conditions. Metronidazole is the standard treatment.

**Velvet:** Dusty golden coating on body. Treat with copper (carefully) and raise temperature.
